</span>CHAPTER X<br/>
RIDING THROUGH THE LAND OF GOSHEN<br/>
<br/>
ANY visitor having studied all that we have hitherto<br/>
seen of Egypt may not unnaturally pretend to some<br/>
knowledge of the country and its people. His knowledge, however, is only partial, and the people he<br/>
has mixed with should not be accepted as entirely<br/>
typical.<br/>
<br/>
Beyond everything else, Egypt is an agricultural<br/>
country, and the "fellahin" (or "soil-cutters," as the<br/>
word means), who number about four-fifths of the total<br/>
population, are its representative class. Descendants of<br/>
the ancient Egyptians, in type and feature the fellahin<br/>
closely resemble the sculptured figures of the monuments, and, in spite of periodical mixtures of other<br/>
blood, remain to-day practically the same as they were<br/>
three thousand years ago.<br/>
<br/>
Among these hard-working agriculturalists is preserved much of that old-world courtesy and simplicity<br/>
of religion which counts hospitality to the stranger<br/>
among its imperative virtues.<br/>

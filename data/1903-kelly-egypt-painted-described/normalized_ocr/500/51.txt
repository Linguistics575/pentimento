CHAPTER II
CAIRO

F EW people carry away any very definite first impress-
sion of Cairo. Its life is so varied and its interests are so
diverse that the new-comer hardly knows upon what to
focus his attention. East and West continually jostle
each other in streets where strange types and costumes
vie with unfamiliar and picturesque architectural features
in their claim for notice. Everyivhere is a sparkling
movement which can only be likened to an ever-
changing series of kaleidoscopic views whose colour-
pictures bewilder while they delight the eye.

Cairo may be roughly divided into two distinct
and widely differing parts-Alle one consisting of the
Esbekiyeh, Ismailia, and Kasr-el-Dribara quarters,
built for, and almost entirely occupied by Europeans; r',
and the other, the purely native town, whose streets
and bazaars, mosques and palaces, have remained prac-
tically unchanged for centuries.

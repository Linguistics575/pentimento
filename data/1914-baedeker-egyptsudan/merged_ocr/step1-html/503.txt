On each side of the Nile stretches a wide belt of fertile land,<br/>
bounded both on the E. and W. by ranges of hills, overtopped by<br/>
<span style="color: #0000FF;">finely </span>shaped peaks, especially on the E., where the ridge retires<br/>
farther from the stream than on the W. The verdant crops and<br/>
palms which everywhere cheer the traveller as soon as he has quitted<br/>
the desert, the splendid hues that tinge the valley every morning<br/>
and evening, the brilliant, unclouded sunshine that <span style="color: #ff0000;">bathes </span>every<br/>
object even in the winter-season, lend to the site of ancient Thebes<br/>
the appearance <span style="color: #0000FF;">of </span><span style="color: #0000FF;">a </span>wonderland, richly endowed with the gifts of<br/>
<span style="color: #0000FF;">never-failing </span>fertility. Most of the <span style="color: #0000FF;">ruined </span>temples are situated in<br/>
the level district and are reached by the waters of the Nile when<br/>
the <span style="color: #0000FF;">inundations </span>are at their highest; while the tombs are hewn in<br/>
the flanks of the hills, where their dark openings are so numerous,<br/>
that the E. slope of the Libyan range might be aptly compared to<br/>
a piece of cork or to a honeycomb.<br/>

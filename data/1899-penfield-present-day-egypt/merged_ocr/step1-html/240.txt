<span>CHAPTER VII<br/>
ISMAIL PASHA AS KHEDIVE AND EXILE<br/>
<br/>
UT for two great errors of judgment, Ismail<br/>
might have ended his days in Cairo, as Egypt's<br/>
khedive, instead of in Constantinople, an exile.<br/>
The first and greater of these mistakes was the<br/>
exaggerated estimate formed of the resources of<br/>
Egypt. Coming to power at a time when the<br/>
prices of agricultural produce, and especially of cotton, were unduly inflated by the Civil War in the<br/>
United States, he sprang to the conclusion that he<br/>
could indefinitely draw a vast tribute from the Nile<br/>
land. <span style="color: #0000FF;">He </span>claimed proprietorship of twenty per<br/>
cent. of the cultivable acreage of the country, and<br/>
what was not his belonged to his subjects. These<br/>
beings, he argued, were his indisputably, soul, body,<br/>
and all that belonged to them. By this process of<br/>
reasoning the doughty pasha felt that "Ismail"<br/>
and "Egypt" were synonymous words.<br/>
<br/>
<span style="color: #0000FF;">His </span>second error was that of exciting the jealousy and ill will of the Sultan, his political master.<br/>
Had Ismail been properly advised, neither of these<br/>
mistakes would have been made, and his history<br/>
would not be so fraught with pathetic contrasts.<br/>

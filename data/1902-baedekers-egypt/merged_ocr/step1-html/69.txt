V. TREES AND PLANTATIONS. During the last thirty or forty<br/>
years trees have been <span style="color: #0000FF;">so </span>extensively planted that Egypt now presents a more richly wooded appearance than formerly. In ancient<br/>
times every square foot of arable land seems to have been exclusively devoted to the cultivation of industrial <span style="color: #0000FF;">crops</span>, the natives<br/>
preferring to import from foreign countries the timber they required<br/>
for ship-building purposes, and probably also the small quantity<br/>
employed in the construction of their temples. Mohammed'Ali,<br/>
a great patron of horticulture, at one time offered prizes for the<br/>
planting of trees, but his efforts were unattended with success, as<br/>
the climatic and other <span style="color: #ff0000;">diffieulties </span>attending the task were then <span style="color: #0000FF;">but<br/>
</span>imperfectly understood in Egypt. <span style="color: #0000FF;">Ibrahim </span>followed the example of<br/>
his predecessor, <span style="color: #0000FF;">bat </span><span style="color: #0000FF;">Abbas </span>I. and <span style="color: #ff0000;">Sa'til </span>were sworn enemies to trees<br/>
of every kind, and they were content that their palaces should be<br/>
exposed to the full <span style="color: #0000FF;">glare </span>of the sun. The Khedive Isma'il, however,<br/>
at length revived the plans of his celebrated ancestor, and by the<br/>

<span style="color: #0000FF;">lebbek </span>(<span style="color: #0000FF;">Albizzia </span>Lebbek), which has long been erroneously called by<br/>

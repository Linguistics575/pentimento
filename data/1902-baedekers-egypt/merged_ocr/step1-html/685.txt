</span>The Anglo-Egyptian Sudan (pp. xxxi, <span style="color: #ff0000;">cii</span>), occupying an area<br/>
approximately equal to that of Central Europe, extends from a line<br/>
drawn at <span style="color: #0000FF;">Fares </span>(p. <span style="color: #0000FF;">376</span>), above <span style="color: #0000FF;">Wadi </span>Halfa, on the <span style="color: #ff0000;">N</span><span style="color: #ff0000;">,</span>, to beyond<br/>
the tenth parallel of latitude on the S. It includes the provinces of<br/>
<span style="color: #0000FF;">Dongola</span>, Berber, <span style="color: #ff0000;">Khartiim</span>, Kassala, <span style="color: #ff0000;">Rmiloftsm</span>, Fashoda, and <span style="color: #ff0000;">Senndar</span>, and the smaller districts of <span style="color: #0000FF;">Wadi </span><span style="color: #0000FF;">Halfa </span>and <span style="color: #0000FF;">Suakin</span><span style="color: #0000FF;">. </span>The<br/>
British and Egyptian flags <span style="color: #0000FF;">fly </span>side by side in the <span style="color: #0000FF;">Sudan</span>, and the<br/>
rights of the joint possessors are defined by a convention signed on<br/>
<span style="color: #ff0000;">Jan. </span><span style="color: #ff0000;">19th</span>, 1899. The interest on the Sudan war-loan (p. ciii) is<br/>
guaranteed to the British Empire, while Egypt bears the <span style="color: #ff0000;">cost </span>of<br/>
the army and is responsible for any <span style="color: #ff0000;">defieit </span>(<span style="color: #0000FF;">IE </span>98,000 in 1900).<br/>
The army consists of 16 battalions of Egyptian troops and a few<br/>
companies of British soldiers. The Governor-General and <span style="color: #0000FF;">Sirdar </span>of<br/>
the Egyptian Army is a British <span style="color: #ff0000;">offieer</span>, appointed by the Khedive<br/>
on the recommendation of the British government, without whose<br/>
consent he may not be dismissed. The Mudirs (p. xxxii) of the <span style="color: #ff0000;">1lrovinces </span>are British officers also<span style="color: #ff0000;">, </span>At present military government and<br/>
martial law prevail throughout the Sudan, though a civil court of two<br/>
instances was <span style="color: #0000FF;">established </span>at <span style="color: #ff0000;">Khartihn </span>in March, 1901 (<span style="color: #ff0000;">comp</span><span style="color: #ff0000;">. </span><span style="color: #ff0000;">p. </span>xx).<br/>
An ad valorem tax of 20 per cent is levied upon the export of gum<br/>
(400 tons in 1900), india-rubber, ivory, and <span style="color: #0000FF;">ostrich-feathers</span>;<br/>
certain articles <span style="color: #0000FF;">of </span><span style="color: #0000FF;">general </span>consumption are taxed 10 per cent of their<br/>
value in the towns; and among the other sources of revenue are taxes<br/>
upon date-palms, irrigation-wheels, and boats, the <span style="color: #0000FF;">pedlars</span>' tax,<br/>
prospecting and mining licences, the house tax, and the land sale<br/>
tax. The considerable <span style="color: #0000FF;">import </span>trade in cotton goods and articles<br/>
for barter with the natives is in the hands of Greek and Arab<br/>
merchants. Beside the Egyptian coins the Maria Theresa dollar,<br/>
equivalent to about 20 pias., is <span style="color: #0000FF;">current</span><span style="color: #0000FF;">.<br/>

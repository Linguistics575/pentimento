 

Arrival in Cairo.

On nearing Cairo, Mount Mokattam and the Libyan
chain of mountains, the profile of the Citadel and its
two elegant minarets with dark blue background, be-
come more and more distinct.

A glimpse of the pyramids may also be obtained on
the right hand some time before reaching Cairo.

The railroad on either side is lined by beautiful gardens,
the houses become more numerous, and the train enters
the splendid new Cairo terminus which is built in Arabes-
que style, and recently finished at a cost of about 60,000
pounds.

 

The City.

On the east bank of the Nile, and ten miles south of
the point where the Rosetta and Damietta branches of
the Nile divide, rises Cairo, the Capital of Egypt, called
by the Arabs simply Masr or Masr-eI-Kahira (Masr
the Victorious, because the planet Masr or Kahir was
visible on the night of its foundation).

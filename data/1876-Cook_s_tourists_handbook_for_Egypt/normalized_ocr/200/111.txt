THE MOSQUES.'

The Mosques of Cairo are in number between 400 and
Soo. Many of them are in a very ruinous condition. There
is no difficulty about admission to the most celebrated, on
payment of a small fee.

For information as to the general arrangements of
Mosques, their officials, services, etc., see p. 44 in the
Introduction.

The oldest Mosque in Cairo is that of Tooloon. It was
built by Ahmed Ebn Touloun, a century before Gowher
built Cairo (p. 86), and stood outside the city till Saladin
included it within his walls. It took three yearsiu building.
at a cost of £72,000. It is a copy of the Kaaha at Mecca,
and a true type of the earliest Mosques. At one time there
was a College in this building, with nine Professors.'lhe
Mosque stands on a hill, which Muslim tradition asserts is
the hill on which the ark rested after the flood, and subse-
quently the spot where Abraham was about to offer Ishmael
(not Isaac) when a ram was sent to be offered instead.
